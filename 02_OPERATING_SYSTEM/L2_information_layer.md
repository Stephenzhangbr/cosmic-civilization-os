# Layer 2 — Information Layer (L2)

## Overview

The Information Layer defines how reality is represented, verified, and protected from manipulation.

This layer is the foundation of all higher-level systems (NCM, Governance, Coordination).

If this layer fails, the entire civilization system collapses into simulated reality.

---

## Core Principle

> Not all data is reality.  
> Only verifiable, physically anchored data can enter the system.

---

## 1. Definition of Real Data

Real Data must satisfy at least one of the following conditions:

### 1. Physical Anchor

Data must correspond to a measurable physical event:

- energy consumption  
- material transformation  
- biological signals  
- hardware state changes  

---

### 2. Irreversibility

The data must be linked to a process that cannot be perfectly reversed:

- entropy increase  
- resource depletion  
- time-bound physical processes  

---

### 3. Multi-Source Consistency

The data must be independently observable from multiple sources:

- different sensors  
- different locations  
- different system architectures  

---

## Invalid Data (Rejected by Default)

The following are NOT considered real:

- purely simulated outputs  
- unverifiable AI-generated content  
- data without physical linkage  
- single-source unverified inputs  

---

## Key Insight

> Data becomes "real" only when it resists manipulation.
> ### 4. Cross-Domain Correlation (Optional Enhancement)

Real data gains higher reliability when it produces consistent effects across multiple physical domains.

Example:

- energy systems  
- thermal signals  
- spatial movement  

The more independent physical domains a data point affects, the harder it becomes to falsify.
## Input Control Principle

No automated system is allowed to inject data into Layer 2 without verification.

All inputs must pass:

- physical anchoring  
- or multi-source validation  

Unverified automated outputs are treated as non-real by default.
---

## 2. Verifiability

Data is considered verifiable only if it can be independently confirmed without relying on a single source or authority.

---

### 1. Independent Replication

A data point must be reproducible by separate observers or systems:

- different sensors  
- different operators  
- different architectures  

If replication fails, the data is downgraded or rejected.

---

### 2. Physical Consistency

The data must be consistent with known physical constraints:

- energy conservation  
- material limits  
- temporal continuity  

Any violation indicates corruption or simulation.

---

### 3. Source Separation

Verification must not rely on the same logical or computational origin.

For example:

- AI model validating its own output is invalid  
- mirrored systems without independence are invalid  

---

### 4. Verification Cost

True verification requires non-zero cost:

- time  
- energy  
- resource usage  

Zero-cost verification is treated as unreliable.

---

## Key Insight

> If a claim cannot be independently verified, it does not exist in the system.
---

## 3. Unforgeability

Data is considered unforgeable when the cost of falsification exceeds the potential benefit of manipulation.

---

### 1. Physical Cost Binding

Each valid data point must be linked to a measurable physical cost:

- energy expenditure  
- material usage  
- time delay  

Data without cost linkage is considered forgeable.

---

### 2. Cross-Domain Resistance

Forgery becomes exponentially harder when data affects multiple physical domains:

- energy systems  
- thermal signatures  
- spatial movement  

Single-domain data is considered weak and subject to challenge.

---

### 3. Temporal Anchoring

Data must be bound to irreversible time progression:

- sequence continuity  
- non-reversible processes  
- decay or depletion  

Replayable or perfectly reversible data is considered untrusted.

---

### 4. Asymmetric Difficulty

Verification must be easier than falsification:

- low cost to check  
- high cost to fake  

If falsification and verification have similar cost, the system becomes unstable.

---

## Key Insight

> Truth is not what can be proven.  
> Truth is what cannot be cheaply faked.
