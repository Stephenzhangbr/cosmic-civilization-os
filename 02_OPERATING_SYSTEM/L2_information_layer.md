# Layer 2 — Information Layer (L2)

## Overview

The Information Layer defines how reality is represented, challenged, and protected from manipulation.

This layer is the foundation of all higher-level systems (NCM, Coordination, Governance).

If this layer fails, the civilization collapses into a controllable simulation.

---

## Core Principle

> Reality is not verified.  
> Reality is continuously challenged.

> Not all data is reality.  
> Only data that survives adversarial interaction is considered real.

---

# 1. Definition of Real Data

A data point is considered REAL only if it satisfies a combination of the following constraints:

---

## 1.1 Physical Anchor

Data must correspond to measurable physical processes:

- energy consumption  
- material transformation  
- biological signals  
- hardware state changes  

---

## 1.2 Irreversibility

Data must be linked to irreversible processes:

- entropy increase  
- resource depletion  
- time-bound transitions  

---

## 1.3 Multi-Source Consistency

Data must be observable across multiple independent sources:

- different sensors  
- different locations  
- different architectures  

---

## 1.4 Cross-Domain Correlation

Data gains credibility when affecting multiple physical domains:

- energy  
- thermal  
- spatial  
- biological  

---

## 1.5 Data Lineage (Mandatory)

All data must include a traceable causal path:

- externally derived origin (never self-declared)  
- transformation steps  
- transmission path  

Data without verifiable lineage is downgraded or rejected.

---

## Invalid Data

The following are rejected by default:

- purely simulated outputs  
- unverifiable AI-generated data  
- self-originated claims  
- single-source inputs without validation  
- data without lineage  

---

## Key Insight

> Data is not real because it exists.  
> Data is real because it resists substitution.

---

# 2. Verifiability

Data must be independently confirmable under adversarial conditions.

---

## 2.1 Independent Replication

Data must be reproducible by:

- independent sensors  
- independent operators  
- independent architectures  

---

## 2.2 Physical Consistency

Data must obey physical constraints:

- energy conservation  
- material limits  
- temporal continuity  

---

## 2.3 Source Separation

Verification must avoid shared origins:

- no self-validation  
- no mirrored systems  
- no shared control layer  

---

## 2.4 Verification Cost

Verification must incur real cost:

- time  
- energy  
- physical resources  

Zero-cost verification is considered invalid.

---

## 2.5 Independence Requirement

Multi-source validation must include structural independence:

- different hardware roots  
- different supply chains  
- different control domains  
- adversarial or non-aligned incentives  

Sources that can be jointly controlled are treated as one.

---

## Key Insight

> Repetition is not verification.  
> Only independent disagreement produces truth.

---

# 3. Unforgeability

Data is considered unforgeable when falsification cost exceeds benefit.

---

## 3.1 Physical Cost Binding

Each data point must be linked to:

- measurable energy  
- material cost  
- time delay  

---

## 3.2 Cross-Domain Resistance

Forgery difficulty increases across domains:

- multi-system coupling  
- non-linear interactions  

---

## 3.3 Temporal Anchoring

Data must be bound to irreversible time:

- sequence continuity  
- non-reversible processes  

---

## 3.4 Asymmetric Difficulty

- verification = low cost  
- falsification = high cost  

---

## 3.5 Temporal Inconsistency Check

Real systems exhibit natural delay and noise:

- domain-specific latency  
- imperfect synchronization  

Perfect cross-domain synchronization is treated as suspicious.

---

## Key Insight

> Perfect consistency is often artificial.  
> Reality contains friction.

---

# 4. Active Reality Challenge

The system must actively challenge reality instead of passively observing it.

---

## 4.1 Environmental Entropy Injection

The system introduces unpredictable physical disturbances:

- thermal fluctuations  
- energy perturbations  
- timing variations  
- random load changes  

---

## 4.2 Distributed Entropy Requirement

All entropy must be generated from multiple independent physical sources:

- quantum processes  
- atmospheric or thermal noise  
- geographically distributed systems  

Entropy must be:

- non-centralized  
- non-reproducible  
- non-precomputable  

If entropy is predictable or controllable, all derived verification is invalid.

---

## 4.3 Non-Replayable Interaction

Challenges must be:

- non-repeatable  
- time-bound  
- non-precomputable  

---

## 4.4 Causal Response Validation

The system evaluates:

- propagation of disturbances  
- cross-domain effects  
- consistency with physical constraints  

---

## Key Insight

> Reality is not proven.  
> Reality is stress-tested.

---

# 5. Anti-Exhaustion Mechanism

The system must resist resource-based attacks.

---

## 5.1 Data Staking Requirement

All data sources must commit resources:

- energy  
- time  
- economic stake  

---

## 5.2 Dynamic Cost Scaling

Suspicious or high-frequency sources face:

- exponentially increasing validation cost  
- reduced priority  
- temporary isolation  

---

## 5.3 Verification Budget Protection

The system must:

- limit verification throughput  
- prioritize high-impact signals  
- degrade gracefully under load  

---

## Key Insight

> The system must not collapse while searching for truth.

---

# 6. Origin Integrity Constraint

Origin cannot be self-declared.

---

## 6.1 External Origin Derivation

Origin must be derived from:

- independent systems  
- external observers  
- cross-source correlation  

---

## 6.2 Multi-Party Origin Binding

Origin must be constructed using:

- multiple independent inputs  
- time-locked signals  
- environmental noise  

---

## 6.3 Non-Rewritable History

- origin is append-only  
- lineage cannot be modified  

---

## Key Insight

> History is not written.  
> History is accumulated.

---

# 7. Non-Linear Cross-Domain Validation

Validation must cross weakly coupled domains.

---

## Principle

Validation must include:

- indirect relationships  
- non-obvious correlations  
- domain mismatch testing  

---

## Key Insight

> Strong correlations can be forged.  
> Weak correlations reveal reality.

---

# 8. Hardware Trust Constraint

All hardware is assumed compromised.

---

## 8.1 No Single Trust Source

- no trusted sensor  
- no trusted manufacturer  

---

## 8.2 Cross-Hardware Requirement

Critical data must rely on:

- different hardware classes  
- different physical designs  

---

## 8.3 Adversarial Supply Graph

Hardware sources must:

- compete economically  
- lack unified control  
- maintain structural conflict  

---

## 8.4 Partial Trust Only

Hardware provides probabilistic signals, never absolute truth.

---

## Key Insight

> Trust emerges from conflict, not authority.

---

# 9. Dual-Track Reality Processing

The system must balance speed and accuracy.

---

## 9.1 Fast-Track Channel (Survival Layer)

Critical signals are processed with:

- rapid plausibility checks  
- minimal validation  
- immediate provisional response  

---

## 9.2 Deep Verification Channel

All signals undergo asynchronous full validation:

- lineage  
- independence  
- cross-domain checks  

---

## 9.3 Reconciliation Mechanism

If contradictions occur:

- rollback or compensate  
- update trust weights  
- adjust system response  

---

## Key Insight

> Survival requires speed.  
> Truth requires time.

---

# 10. Trust vs Stake Separation

Economic stake does not determine truth.

---

## 10.1 Multi-Dimensional Trust

Trust is based on:

- historical accuracy  
- causal contribution  
- independence  
- long-term behavior  

---

## 10.2 Stake Limitation

Stake affects:

- participation  
- priority  

Stake does NOT determine truth.

---

## 10.3 High-Impact Data Isolation

Critical data must undergo:

- mandatory delay window  
- physical verification  
- multi-layer validation  

---

## Key Insight

> Wealth can buy attention.  
> It cannot buy truth.

---

# 11. Threat Model

The system assumes:

- sensor manipulation  
- coordinated fabrication  
- supply chain infiltration  
- reality substitution  

---

## Key Insight

> The system operates under adversarial reality.

---

# Final Principle

> Truth is not what can be measured.  
> Truth is what cannot be cheaply controlled.

---

# Final Note

Layer 2 does not guarantee absolute truth.

It defines a system in which:

- falsification becomes prohibitively expensive  
- manipulation requires systemic control  
- reality must continuously withstand adversarial pressure  

Security emerges not from certainty,  
but from the increasing cost of deception.
